The AWS SysOps Administrator (SOA-C03) Is the Exam Nobody Talks About. That's a Mistake.

Published April 4, 2026 · 6 min read

Everyone wants to talk about the SAA-C03. Every study group, every Reddit thread, every LinkedIn post — it's Solutions Architect this, Solutions Architect that. Meanwhile, the SOA-C03 sits quietly in the corner, being arguably the most practical AWS certification you can get, and almost nobody recommends it first.

I think that's a serious mistake. Let me explain why.

What the SOA-C03 Actually Is

The AWS Certified SysOps Administrator – Associate (SOA-C03) is a 130-minute exam with 65 questions. You need a scaled score of 720 out of 1000 to pass. The cost is $150 USD, same as every other AWS Associate-level cert.

But here's what makes it different from the SAA-C03 or DVA-C02: this exam has exam labs. Not just multiple choice. Actual hands-on scenarios where you log into an AWS console and do things. Configure a CloudWatch alarm. Set up an S3 bucket policy. Fix a broken Auto Scaling group.

When I tell people this, half of them get excited and half of them panic. Both reactions are reasonable.

The 7 Domains (And Where People Fail)

The SOA-C03 covers seven domains, and the weighting matters more than people realize:

  1. Monitoring, Logging, and Remediation — 20%. This is CloudWatch, CloudTrail, EventBridge, and Config. If you can't set up an alarm and an SNS notification in your sleep, you're not ready.
  2. Reliability and Business Continuity — 16%. Backup strategies, disaster recovery, Multi-AZ vs. Multi-Region. They want you to think about RTO and RPO, not just memorize definitions.
  3. Deployment, Provisioning, and Automation — 18%. CloudFormation, Elastic Beanstalk, Systems Manager. The labs often come from this domain.
  4. Security and Compliance — 16%. IAM deep dives, KMS, certificate management. They don't just ask "what is IAM" — they give you a broken policy and ask you to fix it.
  5. Networking and Content Delivery — 18%. VPC, Route 53, CloudFront, ALB/NLB. The networking questions on the SysOps are harder than on the SAA-C03. I'm not joking.
  6. Cost and Performance Optimization — 12%. Right-sizing, Reserved Instances, Savings Plans, Cost Explorer. The smallest domain but the questions can be surprisingly tricky.

Most people underestimate Domain 1 and Domain 3. The monitoring questions aren't just "what does CloudWatch do?" — they're "here's a scenario where your application is throwing 5xx errors intermittently at 3 AM, what's the most operationally efficient way to detect and auto-remediate this?"

Why This Cert Matters More Than the SAA-C03 for Some People

Here's my hot take: if you're going into a cloud operations role — SRE, DevOps, platform engineering, cloud admin — the SOA-C03 is more relevant than the SAA-C03.

The Solutions Architect exam tests whether you can design systems. The SysOps exam tests whether you can keep them running. In my experience, companies care a lot more about the second thing than the first, especially once you're actually in the role.

I've seen people with SAA-C03 certs struggle in operations roles because they never learned how to actually troubleshoot a failing ELB health check or figure out why a CloudFormation stack is stuck in UPDATE_ROLLBACK_FAILED. The SysOps exam forces you to learn that stuff.

The Exam Labs: What to Actually Expect

The labs are the scariest part for most people, but honestly? They're also the fairest part of the exam.

You get access to a real AWS console (a sandboxed one). You're given a task — something like "create an S3 bucket with versioning enabled and a lifecycle policy that transitions objects to Glacier after 90 days." You do the thing. The system checks your work.

A few things I wish someone told me before I took it:

  • The labs are timed separately from the multiple-choice portion. Don't rush the MC to save time for labs — they have their own clock.
  • You can't go back to the MC after starting labs. Finish your multiple choice first.
  • The labs typically test basic operational tasks, not complex architectures. If you've spent any time in the AWS console doing real work, you'll be fine.
  • Practice in the real console. Not just reading about it. Actually create stuff, break it, fix it.

My Honest Study Strategy

I studied for about 8 weeks, roughly 1-2 hours a day. Here's what I'd recommend:

Weeks 1-3: Go through a structured course (Stephane Maarek or Adrian Cantrill both have solid SysOps content). Focus on understanding, not memorizing.

Weeks 4-5: Hands-on labs. Use the AWS Free Tier. Set up monitoring, create CloudFormation stacks, break things on purpose and fix them. This is where most of your learning happens.

Weeks 6-7: Practice exams. You want to be consistently scoring 75%+ before booking your real exam. ExamCert has free practice questions that match the real exam format pretty closely.

Week 8: Review weak areas. For most people, that's either networking (VPC peering, transit gateway) or automation (CloudFormation intrinsic functions, cfn-init).

The Career Angle Nobody Mentions

Here's something interesting: job postings that mention "SysOps" or "CloudOps" have increased 34% year-over-year according to recent data. But the number of people taking the SOA-C03 hasn't kept up. Supply and demand, folks.

If you already have the SAA-C03, adding the SOA-C03 makes you a much stronger candidate for any operations-focused role. It signals that you don't just know how to draw architecture diagrams — you know how to keep production running at 3 AM when everything is on fire.

And if you're deciding between the two as your first cert? Ask yourself this: do you want to design cloud systems, or do you want to run them? There's no wrong answer, but your cert should match your actual career direction.

Common Mistakes That Fail People

  • Studying for the SOA-C03 like it's the SAA-C03. Different exam, different focus. The SysOps exam is more operational, more troubleshooting, more "what went wrong and how do you fix it."
  • Ignoring the labs. You can pass the MC portion and still fail if you bomb the labs. Practice in the actual console.
  • Memorizing services instead of understanding workflows. They don't care if you can list all CloudWatch metrics. They care if you can build an alarm → SNS → Lambda pipeline to auto-remediate an issue.
  • Skipping Systems Manager. SSM is everywhere on this exam. Run Command, Patch Manager, Parameter Store, Session Manager. Know them cold.

The SOA-C03 doesn't test whether you've read the AWS docs. It tests whether you've actually operated AWS infrastructure. There's a big difference.

Is It Worth It?

At $150, with no prerequisite cert required, and with a direct line to some of the highest-demand cloud roles in 2026? Yeah. It's worth it.

The SOA-C03 might not get the Reddit hype or the LinkedIn flex posts. But the people who have it know something the SAA-C03-only crowd doesn't: operations is where the real work happens. And this cert proves you can do it.
