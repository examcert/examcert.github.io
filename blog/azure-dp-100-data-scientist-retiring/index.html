Azure DP-100 Retires June 2026. You Have 2 Months to Get the Data Scientist Cert.

Published March 30, 2026 · 5 min read

Microsoft officially confirmed it: the DP-100 (Azure Data Scientist Associate) exam retires on June 1, 2026. After that date, you can't take it, can't renew it, and can't earn the certification. It's done.

If you've been sitting on this one, your window is shrinking fast. Two months isn't a lot, but it's enough — if you're smart about it.

Why Is Microsoft Killing the DP-100?

Short answer: the Azure AI landscape shifted underneath it. When the DP-100 launched, Azure Machine Learning Studio was the centerpiece. Now Microsoft's pushing hard on Azure AI Studio, Copilot integrations, and the whole responsible AI framework. The exam's content just doesn't match where the platform is heading.

Microsoft hasn't announced a direct replacement yet. There's speculation it'll fold into broader AI engineering paths (think AI-102 territory), but nothing confirmed. Which means if you want "Azure Data Scientist Associate" on your resume, the clock is ticking.

What the DP-100 Actually Tests

The exam has 40–60 questions across 120 minutes. Passing score is 700 out of 1000. Cost: $165 USD. Here's how the domains break down:

  • Design and prepare a machine learning solution (20–25%) — Compute targets, data assets, workspace configuration
  • Explore data and train models (25–30%) — Feature engineering, model selection, hyperparameter tuning
  • Prepare a model for deployment (20–25%) — Pipelines, model registration, responsible AI dashboards
  • Deploy and retrain a model (10–15%) — Managed online endpoints, batch endpoints, monitoring
  • Manage an Azure ML workspace (10–15%) — RBAC, data encryption, compute management

The weighting tells you something important: training and data prep dominate. If you spend all your time on deployment and skip feature engineering, you're going to have a rough exam day.

Why Bother With a Retiring Cert?

Fair question. Here's my honest take:

First, the certification stays on your transcript even after retirement. It doesn't vanish. Recruiters searching for "Azure Data Scientist" will still find it. And since no replacement exists yet, holders of this cert have something genuinely scarce — a credential that can't be earned anymore.

Second, the skills transfer. Everything you learn studying for the DP-100 — Azure ML pipelines, model training, responsible AI — applies directly to whatever Microsoft replaces it with. You're not learning throwaway material.

Third, it's a credibility signal. In a market flooded with people who took a weekend course and call themselves "AI practitioners," having a hands-on data science cert from a major cloud vendor still means something. Maybe more now than ever.

The 8-Week Sprint Plan (Before June 1)

You have roughly 8 weeks. That's tight but doable, especially if you have some Python and basic ML background. Here's what I'd do:

Weeks 1–2: Azure ML Workspace Fundamentals

Get comfortable with Azure Machine Learning Studio. Create a workspace, spin up compute instances, import datasets. Don't just read about it — do it. Microsoft's free sandbox gives you enough compute to practice without spending money.

Weeks 3–4: Data Prep and Model Training

This is the meat of the exam. Focus on automated ML, hyperparameter sweeps, and the Designer (drag-and-drop pipeline builder). Understand when to use each approach. Practice writing training scripts that use the Azure ML SDK v2.

Weeks 5–6: Pipelines and Deployment

Build end-to-end ML pipelines. Deploy a model to a managed online endpoint. Set up batch inference. Know the difference between real-time and batch scoring — the exam loves asking about this.

Weeks 7–8: Responsible AI + Practice Exams

Microsoft has been pushing responsible AI hard. Expect questions about fairness metrics, model explainability, and error analysis. Then hammer practice questions until the patterns click.

Pro tip: The DP-100 includes case study questions that take 15–20 minutes each. Don't save them for the end. Budget time carefully or they'll eat your clock.

Common Mistakes That Fail People

After talking to dozens of people who've taken this exam, patterns emerge:

  • Ignoring the SDK v2. The exam updated to focus on SDK v2 (the azure-ai-ml package). If you're studying from old v1 material, you'll see questions about classes and methods you've never encountered.
  • Skipping the Designer. Yes, it feels simplistic. The exam still tests it. Don't leave free points on the table.
  • Memorizing without doing. This isn't a concepts-only exam. You need to know which buttons to click, which YAML fields to set, which Python classes to instantiate. Hands-on practice is non-negotiable.
  • Underestimating Responsible AI. This section went from "nice to have" to "10–15% of the exam." People who skip it regret it.

Is $165 Worth It for a Retiring Cert?

Here's the math I keep coming back to. Azure Data Scientist roles in the US average $135K–$160K. The cert doesn't guarantee that salary, obviously, but it does get your resume past the keyword filters that block you from even interviewing.

At $165, the cert costs less than dinner for two at a nice restaurant. If it helps you land even one interview you wouldn't have gotten otherwise, the ROI is absurd.

And there's a scarcity angle now. After June 1, no one new can earn this cert. Every month that passes, it becomes rarer. That has a weird kind of value that's hard to quantify.

Final Thoughts

Look — I'm not going to pretend a retiring cert is the smartest move for everyone. If you're brand new to Azure and have no ML background, trying to cram the DP-100 in 8 weeks is going to be painful. Maybe look at the AI-900 as a foundation first.

But if you've got some data science experience, some Azure familiarity, and you've been putting this off? Stop putting it off. June 1 isn't negotiable. Microsoft doesn't do extensions.

Two months. That's what you've got. Make them count.
