I Had Zero Security Experience. 14 Months Later I Had My CISSP. Here's the Brutally Honest Version.

Published February 28, 2026 · 8 min read

Everyone writes about passing the CISSP as if they were destined for it. "I studied for 3 months and passed on my first try!" Good for you. That wasn't my story.

My story involves 14 months, two failed attempts, a crisis of confidence, and the eventual realization that I was preparing for the wrong exam entirely. Not in the "wrong exam code" sense — in the "wrong mental model" sense.

I was a network engineer. My security experience was limited to configuring ACLs and resetting passwords. I had no business taking the CISSP. I took it anyway.

Month 1-3: The Overconfidence Phase

I bought the (ISC)² Official Study Guide — the 1,000-page Sybex book — and started reading. Chapter by chapter, domain by domain. I took notes. I highlighted passages. I felt smart.

Domain 1 (Security and Risk Management) made sense. Policies, governance, compliance frameworks. I could memorize this.

Domain 2 (Asset Security) — data classification, handling requirements. Fine.

Domain 3 (Security Architecture and Engineering) — now we're talking. Encryption algorithms, security models (Bell-LaPadula, Biba, Clark-Wilson), access control mechanisms. I dove deep into the technical content because that's where I felt comfortable.

By month 3, I'd read the entire book. I felt ready.

I was not ready.

Month 4: First Attempt — 600-something

The CISSP CAT (Computer Adaptive Testing) format stopped me at 125 questions. That means I either passed definitively or failed definitively. The screen said "fail."

The exam felt nothing like the book. Every question was a scenario. "You're the CISO. The CEO wants to launch a new product that processes PII. The legal team says GDPR applies. The development team says they can't meet the timeline with encryption enabled. What do you do?"

None of the answer choices were "implement AES-256." They were all about risk acceptance, policy decisions, stakeholder communication, and regulatory compliance. I picked the most technical answer every time.

That was the problem.

Month 5-8: The Reset

I threw out my study approach and started over. Here's what changed:

I stopped thinking like an engineer. The CISSP doesn't want you to configure security. It wants you to manage security. Every question asks "what should you do FIRST?" or "what's the BEST approach?" — and the answer is almost never the technical one. It's the governance one.

Establish policy → assess risk → implement controls → monitor and review. That's the CISSP's entire worldview. If your answer doesn't follow that framework, it's wrong.

I studied the "why" instead of the "what." I used to memorize: "AES uses 128, 192, or 256-bit keys." For the CISSP, what matters is: "When would you choose AES over RSA? What business problem does symmetric encryption solve that asymmetric doesn't?" The exam tests judgment, not recall.

I discovered practice questions that actually match the exam. I burned through three different question banks before finding ExamCert's CISSP practice tests. The scenario-based questions forced me to think like a manager, not an engineer. Every wrong answer had a detailed explanation of why the "CISSP mindset" favored a different choice. At $4.99 lifetime access, I genuinely wish I'd found it before spending $200 on other prep materials that tested memorization instead of judgment.

Month 9: Second Attempt — Still Failed

Yeah. I failed again. But this time, the exam felt different. I understood what was being asked. I just couldn't consistently pick the "most correct" answer when two options both seemed right.

The CISSP is notorious for this. Questions have four answers, two are obviously wrong, and two are both valid — but one is MORE correct based on the CISSP's risk management hierarchy.

After failing the second time, I did something I should have done from the start: I joined a CISSP study group on Discord. Discussing questions with other candidates helped me calibrate my thinking. When I argued for answer B and everyone else picked D, the discussion revealed gaps in my reasoning that I couldn't see alone.

Month 10-14: The Slow Build

I studied 5-6 hours per week. Not the intense 20-hour weeks from before — a sustainable pace. I focused exclusively on practice questions and peer discussion.

My routine: 25 practice questions per day from ExamCert, review every wrong answer with my study group, and write one paragraph explaining the "CISSP thinking" behind each correct answer.

Month 14: Third Attempt — Passed at Question 100

The CAT stopped at question 100. My heart rate was probably 140 bpm. The screen loaded.

"Congratulations."

I sat in the car for ten minutes before I could drive.

What I'd Tell Past-Me

The CISSP is not a security exam. It's a management exam about security. The people who pass quickly are usually managers who already think in terms of risk, policy, and governance. The people who struggle (like me) are engineers who need to rewire their brains.

Three attempts is normal. (ISC)² reports that most people pass on their second or third attempt. The "I passed first try" people on Reddit are a vocal minority.

The experience requirement matters. You need 5 years of security experience (4 with a waiver). But here's what's changing: ISC² is slashing the experience waiver list in April 2026. They're cutting from 50+ accepted certifications down to a handful. If you're planning to use a certification waiver, check whether yours still qualifies.

Start with practice questions, not books. If I could start over, I'd take a diagnostic exam first, identify my weakest domains, and focus there. The free CISSP practice exam on ExamCert would have shown me my gaps in month 1 instead of month 5.

The CISSP Changed My Career

Within 6 months of passing, I moved from network engineering to a security architect role with a 35% salary increase. The CISSP is still the gold standard of information security certifications. It's worth the pain.

But if anyone tells you it's easy? They're either a natural-born manager or they're selling something.

Fourteen months. Three attempts. One certification. No regrets.
