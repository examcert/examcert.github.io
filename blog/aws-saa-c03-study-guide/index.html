How I'd Study for the AWS SAA-C03 If I Had to Start Over

Published February 9, 2026 · 6 min read

The AWS Solutions Architect Associate is probably the most popular cloud certification out there. And for good reason — it opens doors, it pays well, and it forces you to actually understand how AWS services fit together. But there's a lot of bad advice floating around about how to prepare for it.

I've seen people spend six months studying for this exam. I've also seen people pass it in three weeks. The difference isn't intelligence — it's knowing what to focus on and what to skip.

What the SAA-C03 Actually Tests

Let's get the basics out of the way. The SAA-C03 has 65 questions, and you get 130 minutes to finish. The passing score is 720 out of 1000. It costs $150 USD. You can take it at a Pearson VUE center or online from home.

The exam breaks down into four domains:

  • Domain 1: Design Secure Architectures (30%) — IAM policies, encryption, VPC security, network segmentation
  • Domain 2: Design Resilient Architectures (26%) — Multi-AZ deployments, auto scaling, disaster recovery strategies
  • Domain 3: Design High-Performing Architectures (24%) — Choosing the right database, caching, CDN, compute options
  • Domain 4: Design Cost-Optimized Architectures (20%) — Reserved vs. Spot vs. On-Demand, storage tiers, right-sizing

That first domain is the big one. Security alone is almost a third of your score. If you don't know IAM inside and out, you're going to have a rough time.

The Services That Actually Matter

AWS has over 200 services. The SAA-C03 cares about maybe 30 of them deeply, and another 20 at a surface level. Here's where I'd spend my time:

Must-Know Cold

  • EC2 — Instance types, placement groups, pricing models. Know the difference between Dedicated Hosts and Dedicated Instances.
  • S3 — Storage classes (Standard, IA, One Zone-IA, Glacier, Glacier Deep Archive), lifecycle policies, versioning, cross-region replication
  • VPC — Subnets, route tables, NACLs vs security groups, NAT gateways, VPC peering, Transit Gateway
  • RDS — Multi-AZ vs Read Replicas, Aurora specifics, automated backups
  • IAM — Policies, roles, cross-account access, identity federation
  • ELB — ALB vs NLB vs Gateway LB, health checks, sticky sessions

Know Well Enough

  • Lambda, API Gateway, SQS, SNS, EventBridge — serverless patterns come up a lot
  • DynamoDB — partition keys, GSIs, DAX caching, on-demand vs provisioned
  • CloudFront — origin access, signed URLs, Lambda@Edge
  • EFS vs FSx — when to use which file system
  • KMS, Secrets Manager, ACM — encryption at rest and in transit

You don't need to know every single parameter. But you need to know when to pick each service. That's the whole point of being a solutions architect.

My Study Plan (If I Started From Scratch)

Week 1-2: Go through a structured video course. I don't care which one — Stephane Maarek, Adrian Cantrill, or the official AWS Skill Builder. Pick one and stick with it. Don't watch two courses. That's a waste of time.

Week 3: Start doing practice exams. This is where the real learning happens. Every wrong answer teaches you something a video never will. I'd recommend hitting up ExamCert for free practice questions — they cover all four domains and the explanations are genuinely helpful.

Week 4: Review your weak areas. If you keep getting VPC questions wrong, go back and lab it out. Spin up a VPC from scratch — subnets, route tables, internet gateway, NAT gateway, the whole thing. Nothing beats hands-on experience.

Hot take: Reading whitepapers is overrated for this exam. The Well-Architected Framework whitepaper is worth skimming, but don't burn a whole week on documentation. Practice questions are a better use of your time.

Common Traps on the Exam

AWS loves to test your ability to pick the most cost-effective or most operationally efficient solution. Here are patterns I've seen trip people up:

  1. "Least operational overhead" usually means managed services. If the answer choices include managing your own EC2 cluster vs. using a fully managed service, go with managed. Every time.
  2. Multi-AZ vs Multi-Region — Multi-AZ is for high availability within a region. Multi-Region is for disaster recovery across regions. Know the difference.
  3. S3 storage class questions — They love asking about moving data between tiers. Remember: S3 Intelligent-Tiering exists and it's often the right answer when access patterns are unpredictable.
  4. SQS vs SNS vs EventBridge — SQS is for decoupling with queues. SNS is for fan-out (one message to many subscribers). EventBridge is for event-driven routing with filtering rules.

Is the SAA-C03 Worth It in 2026?

Honestly? Yes. Despite what some people say about certifications being "just paper," the SAA-C03 carries real weight. It's the most recognized cloud cert globally. Recruiters filter for it. Hiring managers respect it. And the knowledge you gain studying for it is practical — you'll actually use it on the job.

The ROI is hard to beat. $150 for the exam, maybe $30-50 for study materials (or free if you use resources like ExamCert), and a few weeks of focused study. Compare that to a bootcamp that costs $15,000 and takes three months.

One thing though — don't stop at the cert. Build something on AWS after you pass. Deploy a real application. Set up a CI/CD pipeline. The certification gets you in the door; the hands-on skills keep you there.

Final Thoughts

The SAA-C03 isn't easy, but it's not the monster some people make it out to be. Focus on the high-weight domains (security and resilience are over half the exam), do a ton of practice questions, and don't overthink it. Four weeks of solid, focused study is enough for most people with some cloud background.

If you're brand new to AWS, give yourself six to eight weeks. No shame in that. The point is to actually learn the material, not just memorize answers.

Good luck. You've got this.
